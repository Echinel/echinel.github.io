# Falsification & Steelmanning

Confirmation bias makes you collect evidence *for* a conclusion you already like. The scientific defense is to seek the evidence that would *break* it.

## Practice

1. **Pre-register the kill condition.** Before concluding, write: "I'd be wrong if I saw ____." If nothing could change your mind, you're not reasoning — you're rationalizing.
2. **Hunt for the disconfirming case.** Look for the input, edge case, or counterexample that your explanation can't survive. One genuine counterexample beats ten confirmations.
3. **Steelman the alternative.** State the strongest competing explanation, fairly, and give it a real chance to win. Then show specifically why your conclusion beats it — or switch.
4. **Check for survivorship and selection.** Are you only looking where you expected to find support? What data did you skip?
5. **Prefer the explanation that risks the most** and still survives. A claim that predicts a sharp, checkable outcome is stronger than one that's compatible with anything.

## Quick self-audit

- "What's the single best reason I might be wrong here?"
- "Did I look for a counterexample, or only for confirmations?"
- "Is there a simpler explanation I dismissed too fast?"
- "If a skeptic reran my test, what would they attack — and would it hold?"

A conclusion that has survived a sincere attempt to falsify it is worth far more than one that was never tested.
